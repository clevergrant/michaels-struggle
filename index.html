Chapter 1 — Nǐ yǒu cōngmíng-an menson

My name is Michael. Growing up, my father — an Auditeur with the quiet patience of someone who had listened to machines and people his whole life — would often remind me, "Nǐ yǒu cōngmíng-an menson" — you have a sharp mind.

Most of my life I believed him. Right now, I'm not so sure.

When I think of my early years in Astrothos, I hear the city before I see it: the soft respiration of the Ghost drifting through the viaducts at dawn, the low harmonic thrum of roots adjusting deep below. Buildings yawned awake in shimmering gradients of gold and green, their chromatophores opening to greet the suns. I wasn't the best in school — my mind wandered to anything in motion — but I had friends, and I carried the kind of unblemished confidence you only have when you've never been asked to prove it.

I dreamed of Air Skating and almost nothing else. The idea of another life — a trade, a craft, a quiet post in the archives — seemed distant and absurd. I built my first pair of skates on my thirteenth orbit. That was late for a racer with serious ambitions; the great ones started years earlier, their legs and lungs molded to the work. But at thirteen I could finally hold my focus long enough to learn. My only companion with the same fever for racing was Jens.

Jens was a year older, already ahead in training and in the art of building his Genny — the generator core that gives an Air Skate its lift and personality. I first saw him in one of the root workshops, crouched under the dim violet glow of biolamps, tuning his turbine coils so precisely that even the Ghost lingered in slow spirals, as if watching. His hair was pulled back, sleeves rolled to the elbow, forearms flecked with pale root dust.

Being who he was — always curious, always generous — he noticed me hovering near the threshold and waved me in as though we'd been meeting there for years. He showed me how to shape the intake vanes, how to listen for the whisper of a balanced spin, how to attune a Genny so it matched your gait, your weight, your will. I stayed until the lamps dimmed, and by the time I left I was already planning mine.

From then on, I spent whole days down in those root workshops, surrounded by the scent of damp wood-flesh and the distant heartbeat of the city. My Genny was nothing elegant at first — a patchwork of scavenged rotors, recycled capacitors, and a casing I'd carved myself from a shed plate of biosteel — but it was mine. After only a few days of trial runs, I watched Jens race. Two months later, I was at his side on the Humania circuit, learning how the crowd judged not just speed but balance, artistry, and the flourishes that made the Ghost ripple in approval.

As much as I admired him, there was something about Jens that unsettled me — a quality I could never seem to copy. It wasn't his skill; in the past four years, I'd beaten him more often than not. It was the way he inhabited himself. His confidence wasn't loud, but it was unshakable, and he carried it like an unspoken promise to everyone he met.

He was always happy to see me — genuinely happy — and, as far as I could tell, he was happy to see anyone. Perfectly capturing this strange, infuriating stability was the band around his wrist: a bright, royal-blue khipu. The fiber strands shimmered with a depth of hue that never faltered, each thread braided in the tight, precise pattern only its maker would know. In our culture, you learn not to compare colors — khipus are too personal for that — but I couldn't help it. His was the calm note of a perfect echo. Mine… shifted restlessly, a weather vane in a wind I didn't understand.

Chapter 2 — The Color I Couldn't Hold

My khipu was always a quiet weight on my wrist — not in grams, but in shame. Nobody ever said anything outright, but Jens's khipu seemed cleaner, sharper, brighter than any I had ever seen. Mine never shone with anything close to that kind of color. I'd taken to calling it a dud when no one was listening.

When I let that slip aloud, my family and a few friends would scold me, reminding me that the color didn't matter — that each khipu was personal, a living thread bound to the person who made it. Still, I noticed patterns in my own. My khipu seemed to shift through certain hues when I felt anxious or nervous; other tones appeared when pride bloomed; still others in moments of ease.

Jens's khipu, though, was always the same steady, royal blue. Every race I skated against him — no matter the stakes, no matter how badly I beat him — there it was, shining like a deep note that never wavered. It tormented me, the way he could stay perfectly calm, even in the roar of the crowds during Humania, the Ghost curling luminous around our ankles as we crossed the finish. And why did it have to be so bright? It was as if the city itself wanted me to notice.

Over the years, I began weaving bracelets of my own — soft grass cords and dyed root fibers — to stack along my wrist. It wasn't for style; it was to hide my shame, and the way I shrank beside him. He had the gift of belonging everywhere, knowing everyone; I had Toby and Lehanna. They were older, steadier, their khipus more like mine — restless, uneven, rarely settling into one color for long.

Toby and Lehanna weren't racers. They had a knack for the living systems of the city: pruning the groves, coaxing crops from the greenhouse towers, tending the bioluminescent fungi that brightened the root tunnels. While I was racing or shaving a fraction of weight from my skates, they were elbow-deep in damp bark-flesh, their laughter echoing through corridors that smelled of leaf and loam.

I should explain: Astrothos, the city I call home, lies deep in the Great Dunes. It's alive — grown from the Songseed, fed by the suns, cooled by the Ghost's breath. Every child born here receives a khipu before they can even speak, braided in the ceremony that marks them as part of the whole. Much of what we once knew about them was lost in the Annihilation, but the Auditeurs — our scientists — still study them, hoping to understand every flicker of color, every pulse of light.

My father was an Auditeur, my mother a Canteur. Astrothos seems to like that pairing, perhaps to keep the two from drifting too far into rivalry. Auditeurs chart and probe the city's workings, cataloging every new species the city grows. Canteurs are the keepers of story and song — the ones who ensure that no one forgets where we came from, and why. They carry the echoes of the past forward in Eĥyǔo, woven into Oratia and Exhalia so the city itself remembers.

Some Auditeurs think the Canteurs are sentimental idlers, too willing to accept mysteries instead of solving them. Some Canteurs think the Auditeurs bury themselves in work that misses the heart of what matters. I've heard both speak with their heads lost in the clouds.

I took after my father. That's probably why I was drawn to Air Skating, where one of the unspoken laws is that you build your own skates. Toby and Lehanna were Auditeur-minded as well, happiest with their hands in the living systems. Jens was a proud Canteur — his racing style was pure feel, a sort of dance with the track and the Ghost. I think that's why I often had the edge on him; I would tune my Genny down to the micron, while he trusted whatever shape it had grown into.

When the city began sending volunteers into the wilds — Emissaries to the Enclaves — Jens was among the first to sign up. The idea of living far from the Ghost's familiar light seemed impossible to me; to him, it was an invitation.

He invited me to his farewell gathering, a small thing in one of the high terraces where the Ghost drifts slow and bright at dusk. I told him I couldn't go; Toby and Lehanna had already invited me to a party. I've wondered more times than I can count how different my life might have been if I'd gone to Jens's farewell instead.

Chapter 3 — The Night of the Ghost's Warning

I'll remember the day of the party for as long as I live.

Toby and Lehanna spent most of their hours in the groves, harvesting the endless bounty that grew from Astrothos's living walls and terraces. I loved visiting them there. The groves always felt different from the rest of the city — quieter, though never silent. You could hear the slow respiration of leaf and root, the faint pop and hiss as nutrient pods opened in the soil. They were always happy to see me, smiling through sweat and leaf dust.

That morning, I'd gone for a skate before meeting them, just to watch the endochromatophores awaken. As the suns crested the dunes, the pigments inside every building's skin shifted, catching the light to match weather and power demand. Some turned glassy to flood their interiors with warmth, others drank in heat and darkened to almost black. The air was filled with the first harmonic hums of the city — what the Canteurs call the Echo. Every citizen has an echo of their own, a signature resonance. I'd learned mine well enough to hum along with the city's, the two vibrations mingling until they felt like a single note. By that age, I knew the city's echo better than my own heartbeat.

When I reached the groves, I expected to see Toby and Lehanna bent over the crops. Instead, only a handful of workers moved between rows. Toby's father was among them and told me they had gone back to Lehanna's dwelling. I turned to leave, but the Ghost caught my attention.

In low light, the Ghost manifests as a gauzy, glowing haze that seeps into every street and walkway. Each droplet in that mist holds clusters of bioluminescent dinoflagellates, and when disturbed they bloom with light. Skating through it at speed is like carving trails through liquid starlight; from a distance, you'd look like an apparition haunting the city's arteries.

But the Ghost is more than spectacle. It feels present, aware — as if the city itself is breathing through it. We called it the Ghost because it behaved like another citizen: it knew the streets, the needs of the day, and, in ways that were never officially confirmed but always believed, it knew each of us.

As a child, I learned to trust it. Once, lost in the groves, I'd wandered between identical corridors of foliage until the ground beneath my feet began to glow, marking a path back to my family. Another time, it had led me — for reasons I didn't understand — straight to Jens, who had just finished building a new Genny for his skates. That meeting had changed my life.

And now, it was calling again.

The mist thickened near the granary, where the outer wall was freckled with strange pockmarks. It seemed obvious the Ghost wanted them repaired. I had a pouch of repair proteins with me — meant for patching microfractures in my skates — and I smeared the warm gel into each cavity, watching as the living wall began to knit itself closed. Before they'd sealed even a fraction, the Ghost thinned, drifting away — its way of saying the work was done.

Lehanna's dwelling was clear across the city from the groves, a long walk through arterial walkways where bio-veins glowed faintly beneath translucent walls. She liked the journey. Her grandfather had been the first to chart the network of caves and tubes below Astrothos, and her parents — both Auditeurs — spent most of their lives down there, trying to divine their purpose. Those of us who raced Air Skates used the tunnels as a kind of forbidden circuit.

I had a hunch Toby and Lehanna hadn't gone home at all.

The entrances to the caves sit at the city's outer edge. I slipped into one, less familiar with this branch but confident enough to navigate. The Ghost is thick down there, likely because the dinoflagellates reproduce in the humid dark. The young ones can't control their luminescence; the result is a constant, silvery fog that lights every surface.

I followed a brightening trail until the passage widened into a larger chamber. There they were — crouched over something on the ground, intent and silent.

It was a cluster of mushrooms, their caps a deep, unnatural violet with a faint pulsing glow. Their posture and the scattered tools around them made it clear these weren't a wild discovery. They'd been cultivating them in secret. The pockmarks on the granary wall, I realized, had been from earlier tests. This cavern was their new laboratory. I wondered how they expected to keep anything hidden from the Ghost but kept that thought to myself.

From the way they gestured and traced the curve of each cap, it became clear what these mushrooms were meant to do: they could alter a person's state of mind.

Curiosity won. I took one.

The taste was earthy and metallic, clinging to my tongue. The thick fog of the Ghost swirled tighter, its brightness sharpening until it fractured into impossible geometries — ribbons of light folding in on themselves, tunnels spiraling into colors I had no name for. Shapes shifted at the edge of perception, collapsing into others before I could fix them in memory. The air felt alive, pressing in close.

Then, nothing.

When I woke, I was lying on a sun-warmed street, my mouth tasting of copper and ash. People stood in a wide ring around me, their expressions pulled tight — not in fear, but in the raw alertness that comes after chaos. Later, I would learn that I had lashed out in the throes of the high, striking out at strangers, shoving past others. No one had died, but the damage to trust was done.

By evening, the neighborhood had gathered in the commons. No one shouted, but there was no mistaking the tension in the air — a taut thread holding the group together just long enough to decide what to do. Neighbors spoke in low, deliberate voices, weighing what had happened against the years they had known me. Some asked whether the Ghost would even allow me to stay after such a breach. Others wondered aloud if the city's response to me might change if I showed discipline somewhere beyond its walls.

My parents sat together near the center, listening as much as speaking. My father's voice was calm, but it carried. He said that harm had been done, even if it wasn't my intention, and that repairing it meant more than fixing walls or making apologies. My mother added that an Enclave could be a place to relearn trust — not just the community's trust in me, but my own trust in myself.

The idea settled into the space between us all, quiet but steady, until even those most resistant nodded their agreement. By the time the gathering dispersed, it was no longer a suggestion but a course set: I would go to Dawnreef, a coastal Enclave far from the Ghost's familiar reach, and live there until it was clear to everyone — including myself — that I could return.

The next morning, the Ghost's light along my path out of the city was pale and cool, a neutral farewell.

Chapter 4 — Dawnreef

Dawnreef rose from the ocean like a cluster of living coral, its walls slick with salt and sun, its colors shifting with the tide. The Enclave had been grown from a smaller Songseed, coaxed into its present form by hands and voices far from the reach of the Ghost. Without the familiar bioluminescent haze, the air felt thinner somehow, as if I'd stepped out of a shared dream into the raw glare of waking life. My khipu's threads dulled to a pale, uncertain tone the moment I arrived, and I felt the change like a hollow in my chest.

The people here were warm enough, but there was no undercurrent of recognition, none of the subtle acknowledgments the Ghost provided at home. I was just another newcomer passing through. My parents' reasoning — that an Enclave might help me relearn trust — felt more like an exile than a cure. Still, the city's walls pulsed faintly with energy, the scent of salt and resin in the air, and I tried to imagine this was temporary, a chapter I could close.

It was the sound that drew me first — not the hum of roots or the whisper of leaves, but the sharp, percussive roar of a crowd. I followed it to the edge of the lower terraces, where a track arched out over the water in a series of looping curves and glassy straights. Air Skates hissed and growled along the course, riders leaning into impossible angles, their wake pluming in sheets of mist. But the flourishes I expected — spins, arcs, feather-light reversals — were replaced by collisions. Body met body in deliberate, bone-rattling impact. The crowd didn't cheer for elegance; they roared for takedowns.

That night I found the sign-up lists. The next race was in two days. My name went down without hesitation, though I felt the absence of the Ghost like a missing limb. Here, there would be no subtle encouragement, no shimmer of approval in my peripheral vision. Only the track, the skates, and whoever was willing to drive me into the rails.

When the race began, I tried to give them what I knew — the lines of efficiency, the brief, balanced arcs that had pleased the crowds of Humania. The first time I rolled into a flourish, a shadow slid in at my flank and sent me sprawling with a single, well-placed shoulder. The course skin burned through my gloves. I was back on my feet in seconds, but the message was clear: in Dawnreef, beauty was wasted. The points were for contact.

The rest of the race became a study in avoidance. I skirted wide when packs formed, choosing clear air over combat, letting others expend themselves in collisions. Position by position, I rose through the field, not with speed but with survival. By the final lap I was in second, the leader a few lengths ahead, his posture loose and sure, like someone who knew the track belonged to him.

I drafted behind him down the last straight, every muscle coiled for the final burst. The finish was a ribbon of light across the glassy span. Then — impossibly — he turned. One smooth pivot, skating backward, gaze locked on mine. His leading foot rose and drove into my chest in a standing kick that stole every scrap of speed I'd built. The air left my lungs in a single, shocked sound, and the track met me hard.

By the time I pushed myself upright, the race was over. The leader was gone in a storm of cheers. My khipu had gone nearly colorless, a tangle of pale strands. I sat there long enough for the officials to wave me off the course, the salt air stinging my lungs.

That night, I lay awake in my narrow berth, listening to the Enclave's walls shift and settle with the tide. Outside Astrothos, there were no unspoken agreements about fairness, no invisible net to catch you when you lost balance. Here, the world rewarded force, and those who didn't give it had to find another way to survive.

Chapter 5 — The Heel

Losing in Dawnreef had left a bruise I couldn't see, but I felt it every time I put my skates on. At home, a loss still came wrapped in the Ghost's quiet reassurance — that pale shimmer along the edges of your vision that told you the city had noticed, had weighed your effort and found it worthy. Here, the air was empty. The walls gave nothing back. The only measure of my worth came from the scoreboard, and the scoreboard had no patience for artistry.

I told myself I'd adapt. The crowd's approval here was a different creature — raw, loud, and unashamed of its appetites. They didn't care about grace. They wanted impact. They wanted the sound of two bodies colliding at full speed. And if I wanted that feeling again, the one the Ghost used to give me without effort, I'd have to give them what they came for.

I started small: holding my line instead of yielding, letting a shoulder brush turn into a shove, clipping another racer's trajectory just enough to send them wide. It felt wrong at first — like I was vandalizing something I'd spent years building — but each time the crowd roared, the hollow in my chest filled a little more. Week after week, I learned when to hit, when to vanish, when to bide my time and let others destroy themselves. Slowly, the podium came into reach.

By the end of my second season in Dawnreef, I was winning often enough to stop living in the Enclave's dormitories. I moved into a rented loft above the dry-docks, where the smell of brine and machine oil drifted through the windows. It was nothing like the breathing walls of Astrothos, but it was mine.

The offer came not long after. A representative from one of the circuit's sponsors — a company whose logo seemed to be everywhere except on the winners' podium — pulled me aside after a race. Their proposal was simple: I'd become the heel. The racer the crowd loved to hate. I would lean into my outsider status, wear my Astrothoan colors and patterns, and skate with the cold precision they already found suspicious. I would win just enough to keep them angry, lose just enough to keep them coming back. The pay was more than I'd ever seen.

I said yes.

The transformation was easy. My skates stayed tuned to the same unforgiving precision I'd learned in the root workshops. I wore khipu-styled armbands over my sleeves, deep blue against the rusted steel aesthetic of the diesel-punk locals. While others in the paddock smeared oil across their gear for effect, I kept mine polished and bright, letting the contrast do the talking. The crowd's jeers became part of the show, their boos as addictive as applause.

And I won. More than before, more than I'd dared hope. Each victory felt like a reclamation, a way to prove that Astrothos hadn't sent me here to vanish into the tide.

Then came the out-of-towner. I don't remember hearing his name over the noise, but I knew he wasn't from Dawnreef. His skates were heavy and loud, built for impact, his posture that of someone who'd never finished a race without breaking something. From the first lap, he came for me — hitting early, hitting hard, testing every inch of my balance.

For most of the race, I was holding him off by instinct alone. The aggression I'd cultivated these past months became my only shield. I countered his strikes, cut him off in corners, forced him into the rails when the angles allowed. On the final lap, we were neck-and-neck, the finish a blur of light ahead. He lunged for my flank. I answered with a shoulder-to-shoulder hit so clean it rattled my teeth, buying just enough space to cross the line a fraction ahead.

The crowd's noise broke like a wave. My khipu flared bright for the first time since I'd left home.

When I looked back, he was standing still on the track, helmet in hand, his face carved into something between rage and promise. His mouth formed words I couldn't hear, but I knew their shape: not done.

I walked away riding the high of victory, the air electric in my lungs. But under it all was a weight I couldn't shake — the sense that I'd just crossed into a different kind of race, one where the stakes would be set by someone else.

Chapter 6 — The Night Without the Ghost

The night the Enclave burned began without warning. I woke to the sound of nothing — no wind, no distant hum of generators — just the brittle silence of walls holding their breath. Out past the dry-docks, an orange bloom unfurled against the darkness, then split into smaller, sharper bursts. The sound followed late, a deep concussion that made the glass in my loft tremble.

By the time I reached the balcony, the horizon had become a jagged silhouette of black smoke and embers. The air smelled of salt and burning resin. Sirens, crude and mechanical, tore through the streets. The Enclave was on fire.

I moved without thought, skates in hand, cutting through the narrow, uneven corridors that led toward the Songseed's heart. The closer I came, the more the streets filled with people: some carrying buckets, some dragging injured neighbors, all moving in the direction opposite mine. The smoke thinned only when the walls gave way to the open plaza where the Enclave's central structures once stood.

The plaza was unrecognizable. Sections of the living architecture had gone black and brittle, their colors drained to ashen gray. The smell of burned bio-flesh turned my stomach. In the middle of it all, the emissaries moved with grim precision, guiding survivors to the edges, speaking to those who could barely stand. One of them, a woman I'd spoken to only twice before, caught sight of me and paused.

Her eyes held me still before her words did. Three dead. The out-of-towner from the race had vanished before dawn. One of the dead was Jens.

For a moment I thought I'd misheard her — the name didn't belong here. She told me he'd been in Dawnreef only a few weeks, sent — or perhaps sending himself — to help me return home. He'd been watching me race. Waiting for the right time to speak. I hadn't known. I hadn't even seen him. And now I never would.

I stood there long after she moved on, staring at the charred ruin as if some detail might change the truth. The Ghost was not here to wrap the edges of my grief in light. Only the sharp air of the coast and the slow settling of ash marked the moment.

When the Exhalia began, I joined without hesitation. The survivors formed a circle around the reclamation site, our voices taking shifts to carry the echo forward without pause. We sang in Eĥyǔo, not for the crowd or for each other, but for the city, for Jens, for the three who would now be part of its breath. Hours blurred into days. My voice cracked, healed, and cracked again, but I did not leave.

Somewhere in that unbroken thread of sound, the Enclave began to answer. It was not the Ghost's luminous tether, but something deeper — a pulse that moved below thought. My own voice seemed to braid with it, until the vibration settled into my bones and memory opened like a door.

I was no longer in Dawnreef. I was small again, crouched in the hidden tunnels of Astrothos after the Annihilation, listening to the city breathe while loss hollowed the air. I felt the same ache I had as a child when I first learned that people could be here one moment and gone the next, that the city could take them in but not give them back.

And in that resonance, I recognized something I had never let myself name: the truth that Jens had been part of my home, long before he tried to bring me back to it. That I had carried his presence like the sound of the city's roots — always there, even when I wasn't listening.

When the Exhalia ended, I left without speaking to anyone, the night air pressing cool against my skin. My loft was exactly as I had left it, but it no longer felt like a place to return to.

Chapter 7 — The Leaving That Wasn't

The next race came and went without me. I heard the roar of the crowd from my loft — the same cadence that had once pulled me to the track like a tide — but I stayed seated, staring at the tools on my workbench without moving my hands. Part of me wanted to believe I was above it now, that the loss of Jens and the bombing had shifted my priorities beyond points and podiums. Another part suspected I simply couldn't bear to race as if nothing had happened.

For days, I drifted. The salt air that once felt sharp and alive now carried only a faint taste of ash. I stopped tuning my skates, stopped watching the circuit's feeds. But the out-of-towner stayed in my thoughts, his sneer on the track now fixed to the image of the Enclave's blackened shell. I told myself the only way forward was to face him.

Finding him wasn't difficult. He had the kind of presence that left ripples in every backroom and dockside bar in Dawnreef — always a drink half-finished, always someone eager to say they'd seen him. When I finally found him, leaning against a rust-streaked railing that overlooked the breaker line, he didn't even look surprised.

I didn't tell him what he'd taken from me. I didn't ask why. I simply handed him everything I had earned since arriving in Dawnreef — the winnings, the sponsor payments, the quiet hoard I'd built in my rented loft. Enough to disappear with. Enough to make leaving easy. I asked him to stay away from Dawnreef.

He laughed. A slow, dry sound, like someone testing the edge of a blade. He agreed without hesitation, as if the exchange were a game he'd already won. Then he turned his back to me and let the ocean wind carry him somewhere else.

I walked away with no proof I'd bought anything but his amusement.

The next weeks blurred into work. The Enclave's walls could be coaxed to heal with care, but without the full neural network of Astrothos behind them, the process was slow. I worked alongside emissaries and locals, shaping living supports, tending to regrowth, hauling away what couldn't be saved. It was the kind of labor that had no scoreboard, no spectators, only the satisfaction of seeing something once broken begin to stand again.

Months passed. The plaza stopped smelling of ash. The living structures began to pulse with new color, pale at first, then deeper. When the Enclave could stand on its own, I stayed. The idea of returning to my loft and the circuit felt as hollow as the empty husk of a burned wall.

Instead, I began teaching. Not the Dawnreef style of racing — all elbows and calculated damage — but the lines and flourishes I'd learned in Astrothos. I showed the few who were curious how to listen to their skates, how to tune them until they matched the rhythm of their stride, how to let movement be more than the shortest path to victory. It wasn't popular, but the ones who stayed came back for the same reason I did: to remember there was another way.

In the evenings, when the tide ran low and the air was calm, I would skate the outer track alone. No crowd, no points. Just the sound of the wind over the water and the faint memory of a city's breath I still hoped to hear again.

Chapter 8 — The Road Back

It came to me one morning without ceremony — not as a flash of clarity, but as the quiet, persistent thought that I had done enough here. Dawnreef stood again. The plazas no longer bore the smell of ash, the new growth carried the steady pulse of a place that could heal itself, and the apprentices I'd taken on no longer needed my hands to steady theirs. What they did need, I realized, was to take what I had taught them and make it their own.

The decision to leave was less like a door swinging open and more like the tide withdrawing. I felt it pull at me in every empty lap I skated along the outer track, the air cushions whispering over the course's skin, in the way the horizon never changed no matter how fast I moved.

On the slow walk back to my quarters, I let the thought stretch out, examining it from every angle. I had come to Dawnreef in exile — not chosen, not prepared, convinced that distance from Astrothos meant distance from myself. I had spent my first months here chasing approval in the only form the place offered: points, podiums, the roar of strangers. Then came the race that turned a rival into an enemy, and the night that stripped away the illusions I had been clinging to.

Jens's death had broken something in me, but in its fracture, I had found edges I didn't know I had — the parts of myself that could choose to stay when no one expected it, to work without reward, to teach without measuring who listened. I had learned that an Enclave was more than an outpost of Astrothos; it was a mirror, showing what the city's values looked like when they had to survive without the Ghost's constant presence.

Before leaving, I went to the plaza where Jens's obelisk stood. It rose pale against the rebuilt walls, its surface breathing with slow pulses of light, every shimmer a pattern only the city understood. His mind was now part of Dawnreef — not lost, but woven into its pulse, like a hidden chord in a song that would play long after those who knew him were gone. I thought of the Ghost's reach back home, how it could carry the sense of a person through space like a held breath. Here, there was no Ghost. If I wanted to feel Jens, I had to come to this place, stand in its air, and let the city's echo find me. The knowledge was both an ache and a comfort: Jens belonged to Dawnreef now, and Dawnreef would carry him forward in ways I could not.

The road back to Astrothos would not be the same as the one I had taken out. There would be no crowd at the gates, no ceremony. I didn't expect the Ghost to meet me with any special light. The city might not even notice I'd been gone. But I would notice. I would carry Dawnreef in the way my skates felt under my feet, in the weight of my own restraint, in the absence of shame where it once had lived.

When I finally saw Astrothos again, the walls breathing in their familiar gradients of gold and green, the Ghost drifted at street level in a pale morning haze. Its light curled gently at my ankles, as if leading me home, but without urgency. I walked instead of skating, letting the city's pulse match my own.

My khipu still flickered in restless colors, never holding a single tone for long. I didn't hide it this time.
